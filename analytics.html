Case Study #2

Hypothesis Testing: Political Behavior

Testing whether COVID-19 booster shot rates differ significantly between political affiliations using confidence intervals and statistical significance.

"The 90% confidence interval for the difference in booster rates (2.28% to 11.72%) does not include zero, indicating a statistically significant political divide."

15.0% Democrat Booster Rate
8.0% Republican Booster Rate
7.0% Absolute Difference
90% CI 2.28% → 11.72%

Null Hypothesis

No difference in booster rates between Democrats and Republicans

Test Result

Reject null hypothesis at 90% confidence level

Conclusion

Democrats significantly more likely to receive boosters in this sample

Statistical Methodology

Sample Sizes: 280 Democrats, 200 Republicans

Confidence Level: 90% (Critical z-value: $1.645$)

Standard Error: $0.0287$ for difference between proportions

Analysis: Confidence interval approach for hypothesis testing

Case Study #3

Simpson's Paradox in Public Policy

How Florida appears to have higher overall support for vaping bans, but California actually has higher support in every age group—a classic statistical paradox.

"Florida's overall support appears higher (58% vs 50%), but California actually leads in every age group. The paradox occurs because Florida has more older respondents who generally support bans at higher rates."

58% FL Overall Support
50% CA Overall Support
+10% CA Leads in Age Groups
70% FL 40+ Population

The Statistical Illusion

This is Simpson's Paradox: a trend appears in different groups of data but disappears or reverses when the groups are combined.

Florida Sample Composition

• 70% older adults (higher support for bans)

• 30% younger adults (lower support for bans)

• Older demographic inflates overall percentage

California Sample Composition

• 25% older adults

• 75% younger adults

• Younger demographic depresses overall percentage

Analytical Takeaway

This case demonstrates why examining data at appropriate levels of aggregation is crucial. Relying solely on overall percentages can lead to misleading conclusions when underlying group compositions differ significantly. Proper analysis requires looking at stratified data.

Case Study #4

Confidence Interval Analysis

Calculating precise confidence intervals for educational attainment and college degree proportions from survey data.

"With 90% confidence, the true population proportion of college graduates lies between 35.83% and 40.83%, definitively exceeding the 30% threshold with statistical significance."

90% CI 14.89 → 15.23 years
38.33% Sample Proportion
0.0152 Standard Error
1,020 Sample Size

Statistical Precision

Educational Attainment CI

Mean: $15.06$ years

SD: $3.299$ years

90% CI: $14.89–15.23$ years

Interpretation: Narrow interval indicates precise estimation

College Degree CI

Proportion: $391/1020 = 0.3833$

90% CI: $0.3583–0.4083$

Test: Entire interval > 30%

Conclusion: Statistically significant > 30% threshold

Methodological Rigor

Sample Statistics: Computed using Excel from Gallup survey data

Assumptions: Normal distribution for analytical purposes

Critical Values: $z = 1.645$ for 90% confidence level

Standard Errors: Calculated preserving precision through intermediate steps
