01 — Economic Modeling

EVs: $8,000 Cheaper Over 10 Years

"Federal incentives make EVs cheaper immediately, saving $8,129 over a decade despite higher sticker prices."

Net Present Value analysis comparing Chevy Bolt EV ($29,500) vs Honda Civic ($23,500) reveals the true economic story. With the $7,500 federal tax credit, EVs achieve cost parity on day one. Over 10 years at 4% discount rate, total ownership costs are $28,121 for EV vs $36,250 for ICE—an $8,129 advantage driven by 50% lower annual fuel costs ($755 vs $1,572).

Tools: NPV Analysis, Discounted Cash Flow, Sensitivity Analysis
02 — Financial Sensitivity

Higher Rates Favor EVs

"At 8% discount rate, EV advantage grows to $6,984. Higher rates amplify the value of lower operating costs."

Sensitivity analysis reveals a critical insight: higher discount rates weaken the EV economic case. While both vehicles' total costs decrease in present value terms, the EV's advantage shrinks from $8,129 (4% rate) to $6,984 (8% rate). This occurs because EVs' primary benefit—lower future operating costs—gets discounted more heavily at higher rates, making their higher upfront price harder to justify.

Tools: Sensitivity Analysis, Scenario Modeling, Financial Optimization
03 — Cost Structure Analysis

The Fuel Cost Differential

"EVs save $817 annually on fuel—paying back the price premium in just 3.5 years without incentives."

Annual cost analysis shows the fundamental economic driver: electricity costs $755/year vs gasoline at $1,572/year for average driving (13,476 miles). This $817 annual savings accumulates rapidly. Even without the $7,500 tax credit, the $6,000 price premium pays back in 7.3 years. With incentives, payback occurs immediately, creating positive cash flow from year one.

Tools: Cost-Benefit Analysis, Payback Period, Cash Flow Modeling
04 — Environmental Economics

Clean Air Pays Dividends

"Each EV prevents 14.3 tons of CO₂ over 10 years while saving $8,129—climate action that pays for itself."

Combined environmental-economic analysis reveals powerful synergy: EVs reduce emissions by 68% (20.8 tons to 6.5 tons over 10 years) while delivering substantial financial savings. This creates a compelling value proposition where environmental benefits and economic advantages reinforce each other. In New York's grid (0.55 tons CO₂/MWh), each EV effectively pays $568 per ton of CO₂ avoided through fuel savings alone.

Tools: Emissions Analysis, Environmental Economics, Lifecycle Assessment
